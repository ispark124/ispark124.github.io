And I'm running again. Running down roads I've been down a thousand times over. My feet hitting the pavement, sure of where the next hills, stoplights, and mile markers are. The rhythm of cars, kids getting out of school, and sprinklers are all too familiar—everything on schedule.

It's been at least a six months since I've run this route and at least four years since I've run it regularly, but it feels like I just ran it last week. The roads haven't changed.

*This used to be **novel** once too.* 

In this past month since school's ended and I'm getting ready to leave for work, I've been visiting a bunch of my friends along the east coast while I'm still here. I've been heading into the city about twice a week, popped by Philly, went down the shore, visited school briefly, and even made it down to the DC area. While it's been a lot of driving and traveling, I can't think of a better way to spend my time than visiting the people I love. 

One thing in particular that's really struck me as I've been visiting my friends is visiting their childhood **homes**. I've been driving up to their houses thinking, "These are the streets they've driven down a thousand times over, the neighborhood that's raised them, the town that's shaped who they are." My friend and I have been talking about how going to people's **homes** contextualize so much about a person. We've come to know people outside of the context of their upbringing and now we've been transported back into the same context that made them who they are. The things that are **novel** to me are routine and even comforting for my friends. Even visiting my friends that are temporarily living somewhere, either for school or work, has opened my eyes to their lives. 

I've been invited into these intimate, mundane little vignettes of my friends' lives that reveal more of who they are to me. The path in the grass to the local ice cream shop formed by decades of footprints. The conversations had on the patio that's hosted countless birthday parties and barbecues. The tiny park bench in the city my friend sits in at after work. The local Dunkin Donuts my friend reminisced about. The restaurant another friend works at that serves coffee with a little cookie. All of it a lived-in space that I would've normally just passed by. My friends embody these small moments with them and have been shaped by them. 

*This used to be **novel** once too.*

The roads haven't changed. I'm still running a route I've done since high school, but I pass the restaurant I served at last winter break and smile at the thought of my manager still drinking Turkish tea in her corner of the room. I make my way past an old friend's house that I both grew close to and lost touch with in the last four years. I run past the pickleball courts that my family played at for the first time last summer.

I stopped running with my Garmin these past few months or so. Partly because I'm trying to care less about my pace and obsessively recording everything and partly because I already know where and how far I'm going. I know these streets, these hills, these paths that I've helped form and that have helped form me. In the same breath and stride, I'm realizing how much I'm still being formed by the space around me and the memories I'm still making.

I reach the hill a mile and a half from where I live—the final push before I'm cruising **home**. I'm brought back to the countless mantras I've said to myself over the years to get myself over that hill, and today it's, "You've done this a thousand times over, Isaiah."

I'm thinking about what life will be like when I move. It's been comforting visiting all my friends in the midst of their lives. Everything that's so new to me is nostalgia for them. Everything that will be new to me will soon be nostalgia as well. And once it does, there's more memories to be made still. I'm excited for the **novel**, but I'm more excited for when it becomes **home** a thousand times over.
